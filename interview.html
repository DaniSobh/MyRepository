Master your interview skills

Unlock success in interviews by adopting strategic approaches that highlight your strengths and suitability for the role.

PREPARATION

Pre-Interview

1. Research the company

Before going into the interview, research the company along with its history, values and mission. Check their official website first, then move onto other sources. If there are any client, customer or employee reviews, study them and identify any recurring themes that might alter your decision to work with them. It also prepares you to answer any questions relating to the company, proving to your interview that you familiarized yourself with who the company is.



2. Research your interviewer

Along with researching the company, identify who within the organization might interview you and research them as well. Identify their professional social media profiles and scan through their interests. It often helps to find common ground with your interviewer and bring it up during the interview. For example, if you mention that you both enjoy hiking, you immediately stand out to the interviewer, ensuring they take additional consideration toward you after you leave.



3. Prepare a set of questions

At the end of the interview, most interviewers open up to questions from the interviewee. Having questions prepared at the end often shows that you have a greater interest in the role and want to learn more about the company. Consider questions such as:

  • What is the company culture like?
  • Is there an opportunity for growth in this role?
  • How do you measure employee performance?
  • What is the most challenging aspect of the position?
  • What do you enjoy most about working with this company?


4. Conduct a mock interview

Find someone you trust or who has experience in interviewing and conduct a mock interview. Assign them a series of questions to ask related to your role. Encourage them to find or come up with additional questions as well without telling you what they are. At the end of the interview, take any feedback they have on improving your performance. Once ready, conduct the mock interview again.





5. Print out physical copies of your resume

Even though they have your resume on file, many interviewers prefer seeing a physical copy during the interview. Print several copies out before your interview in preparation for them to ask. Additionally, if they ask any questions directly related to your resume, you can immediately reference it to address their questions or concerns.



6. Eat a healthy meal before the interview

Consider eating a healthy meal several hours before your interview. Consider foods rich in protein, healthy fats and fiber such as:

  • Whole grain toast
  • Avocado
  • Spinach
  • Whole eggs


7. Clean and press your clothes

At least a day before your interview, ensure you have a clean set of clothes. Iron and press formal clothes such as dress pants and dress shirts to free them of wrinkles. Additionally, pack make-up and deodorant for small touch-ups just before your interview, if necessary.



8. Dress for the role

Choose a set of clothes that are appropriate for the role. For best results, choose clothes that are a step above the role's normal standards. For example, if the role requires business casual attire, choose business professional attire for the interview, including a suit and tie or skirt and blouse.




9. Turn your cell phone off before the interview

Bringing your phone into the interview with you is often a necessity. If you need it, consider silencing it or turning it off. Ensure no vibrations or other tones emanate from your phone or interrupt the interview.



10. Prepare for different types of interviews

There are three primary types of interviews. A behavioral interview focuses on how you react in employment-based situations. Most of the questions present specific scenarios and allow you to address how you might handle them. Experiential interviews focus on your previous experience and the decisions you made in the past. The third type of interview is a combination of both behavioral and experiential interviews.



11. Consider stories from previous positions

Interviewers often ask questions that encourage you to describe situations that occurred in previous positions. This gives them a better understanding of how you might react to similar situations that occur within their workplace. Before your interview, think back to your previous positions to memorable situations that you excelled in. Use them as examples to answer certain questions.

During the Interview

Listen Actively

Pay close attention to the interviewer's question. Make sure you understand it fully before answering. If you're unsure about something, don't hesitate to ask for clarification.

Take a Moment to Think

It's okay to take a brief pause to gather your thoughts before answering. This shows that you're thoughtful and not just giving a rushed response.

Answer Concisely

Be clear and concise in your responses. Avoid rambling or going off on tangents. Focus on directly addressing the question asked.

Use the STAR Method

When answering behavioral questions, use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to structure your responses. This helps you provide a well-organized and comprehensive answer.

Highlight Relevant Skills and Experience

Tailor your responses to showcase how your skills and past experiences make you a strong fit for the position. Provide specific examples whenever possible.

Stay Positive and Professional

Maintain a positive attitude throughout the interview. Avoid speaking negatively about previous employers or colleagues. Be polite and professional in your tone and demeanor.

Ask for Clarification

If a question is unclear or if you need more information to answer it effectively, don't hesitate to ask for clarification or request more context.

Engage with the Interviewer

Interviews are not just about answering questions but also building rapport. Engage with the interviewer by making eye contact, nodding, and showing enthusiasm for the role.

Avoid Over-Sharing

While it's important to provide relevant information, avoid over-sharing personal details or going off-topic. Stick to professional topics.

Follow the Interviewer's Lead

If the interviewer is more conversational, you can engage in a discussion. If they prefer structured questions, provide structured answers.

Close Strong

At the end of the interview, express your interest in the position and ask about the next steps in the hiring process. Thank the interviewer for their time and consideration.

Stay Calm and Confident

It's natural to feel nervous, but try to stay calm and confident throughout the interview. Remember that it's also an opportunity for you to evaluate if the company is a good fit for you.

Body Language Matters

Maintain good posture, use appropriate gestures, and make eye contact to convey confidence and professionalism.

Be Honest

Always be truthful in your responses. If you don't know the answer to a technical question, admit it, but express your willingness to learn.

Post-Interview

1. Send a Thank-You Email:

Within 24 hours of the interview, send a personalized thank-you email to each person you interviewed with. Express your gratitude for their time and reiterate your interest in the position. This is also an opportunity to briefly mention key points from the interview that reinforce your qualifications.

2. Review and Reflect:

Take some time to reflect on the interview. Think about your responses and the questions asked. Identify any areas where you could have performed better or provided more detailed answers. This reflection can help you improve for future interviews.


3. Follow Up on Promised Actions:

If you discussed any specific actions during the interview, such as providing additional documents or references, make sure to follow through on these commitments promptly.

4. Continue Your Job Search:

While waiting for a response from the prospective employer, continue your job search. Don't put all your eggs in one basket. Keep applying to other positions and attending interviews if you have them scheduled.


5. Stay Patient:

It can be nerve-wracking to wait for a response after an interview, but it's essential to be patient. The hiring process can take time, and the decision-making may involve multiple rounds of interviews or discussions.

6. Network:

Use this time to expand your professional network. Connect with interviewers and other employees from the company on LinkedIn or other professional networking platforms. Engaging with them can help keep you on their radar


7. Send a Follow-Up Email (if Necessary):

If a reasonable amount of time has passed without an update and you haven't heard back from the employer, it's acceptable to send a polite follow-up email to inquire about the status of your application. Be respectful and express your continued interest.


8. Prepare for a Second Interview:

If you are invited for a second interview, take the time to prepare thoroughly. Review your previous interview performance, research the company and role further, and be ready for more in-depth questions.


9. Maintain a Positive Attitude:

Regardless of the outcome, maintain a positive attitude. Job hunting can be challenging, but a positive mindset is essential for success. If you receive a rejection, use it as an opportunity to learn and grow.


10. Seek Feedback:

If you receive feedback from the employer, whether it's a rejection or an offer, take it constructively. Use any feedback to improve your interview skills and tailor your approach for future opportunities.
